Research

The neurons that make an animal move.

We use the fruit fly to figure out how neural circuits are built during development, and how they turn brain activity into coordinated movement.

Start with the behavior, work back to the circuit.

When an animal moves, its nervous system is choosing an action and timing the muscles to carry it out. In a fly, we can watch a behavior and then trace it back to the specific neurons responsible, which is much harder to do in a bigger brain.

01

Descending neurons

The link between brain and body

Descending neurons are the small set of cells that carry signals from the brain into the nerve cord, where the circuits that actually move the body live. During my PhD I helped identify one of them, the Mooncrawler Descending Neuron: switch it on and a forward-crawling larva stops, reverses, and backs up, turning on backward movement and shutting off forward movement at the same time. At Bucknell we use flies to ask how neurons like this pick one action over another, and how that switch is built into the circuit.

02

Circuit development

Building a circuit before it's used

Before an animal can move, the circuit that produces movement has to be assembled: each neuron has to be born on schedule, take on the right identity, and connect to the right partners. My work here, including studies of the Hunchback transcription factor and the interneurons that keep left and right muscle contractions matched, asks how a circuit's developmental history shapes the behavior it can produce. We follow those steps with fly genetics and imaging.

03

Methods

How we work

We combine Drosophila genetics (GAL4 and split-GAL4 lines that give us handles on specific neurons), optogenetics (light-sensitive proteins like CsChrimson that let us switch a chosen neuron on in a moving animal), confocal microscopy, and quantitative behavior tracking. The through-line is always the same: start from one identified cell type and connect it to something the animal does.

Motor control, from crawling to flight.

The same question runs through very different behaviors: how does a nervous system produce one coordinated movement instead of another? In my postdoctoral work I took it into flight. A fly steers with only about a dozen pairs of wing muscles, so I ran a large optogenetic screen, activating candidate neurons with light in tethered, flying flies, to find the cells that adjust wing motion, then traced them by light and electron microscopy. It is part of why the lab thinks about motor control across scales, from a crawling larva to a fly in the air.
